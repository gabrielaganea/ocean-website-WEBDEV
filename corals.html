The sea is home to the world's largest living structure - the Great Barrier Reef. Measuring around 2,600km, it can even be seen from the Moon!







Corals are marine invertebrates that come in different shapes, sizes and colors. Often mistaken for plants, corals are in fact animals, most closely related to jellyfish and anemones. However, they don't age, they constantly regenerate. Although reefs cover less than 2% of the ocean bottom, one-quarter of all ocean species depend on them.

The large coral reefs seem to be the oldest living animals in the world. Their longevity is 541 to 485.4 million years. Corals can get easily stressed. If the water temperatures rises, corals stress and they turn white. If the condition returns to normal, corals can get back their color.



Many parts of a coral reef can be harvested to make medications. According to the National Oceanic & Atmospheric Administration: "Coral reef plants and animals are important sources of new medicines being developed to treat cancer, arthritis, Alzheimer's disease, heart disease, viruses, and other diseases." The quality of the water is also improved by coral reefs, by filtering out the things that are floating in the ocean's water.

Coral reefs help the marine plants to survive, stabilizing the sea bed and preventing it from getting washed-out while also lessening the impact of storms. Those plants also provide food and protection for countles marine animals.












Coral bleaching increased from 8% in the 1980s to 31% in 2016.

Limiting the future global warming to 1.5C would greatly improve the chances of survival.

Since 2016, more than half of the Great Barrier Reef has died.






Coral Bleaching Timeline





1

1980

FIRST MASS CORAL BLEACHING EVENT. Terry Hughes, director of the ARC Centre of Excellence for Coral Reef Studies stated that "Before the 1980s, mass bleaching of corals was unheard of". Before, these bleaching events were rare, occurring at a rate of once every 25 to 30 years. Now mass coral bleaching events happen about every six years, far too fast for ecosystems to recover.

1997-1998

FIRST GLOBAL EVENT. One of the hottest summers in the 20th century was recorded. Reports of record levels of coral bleaching in the eastern Pacific began to pour in during late 1997. Mild bleaching began in late January of 1998, intensifying by February. During this event 16% of corals reefs have been lost. Most reefs recovered fully.

3

2002

NOT A GLOBAL EVENT. This bleaching event was slightly more severe than the one in 1998. Around 60% of the Great Barrier reef showed bleaching on central and inshore areas. Reports show that the firs signs were in january, but the worst was over in April. Fewer than 5% suffered high mortality, in the Bowen area being the worst affected, where around 70% of corals died.

2005-2006

NOT A GLOBAL EVENT. In 2005, half of all the Caribbean coral reefs in the United States were lost. In January and February 2006, significant coral deaths due to bleaching, around 30-40%, were recorded in the Keppel Islands. The degree of bleaching was worse than in previous years.

5

2010-2011

SECOND GLOBAL EVENT. During this event, the most affected places were in Southeast Asia and Western Australia. For several reefs this was the first recorded coral bleaching, inlcuding the Nigaloo reef. The Great Barrier Reef dodged a bullet during this event. In 2011, bleaching was caused by an influx of freshwater.

2014-2017

THIRD GLOBAL EVENT. The longest and deadliest bleaching event to ever be recorded in history. The event started in the summer of 2014, in the North Pacific. Extensive coral bleaching in Hawaii has been documented in November 2015. In June 2016 the coral morality for the entire Great Barrier reef was at 22%. By 2017, the central third of the Great Barrier Reef was affected.
